You may reference the citation provided by Apte as well as the discussion here: "https://github.com/sanskrit-lexicon/csl-corrections/issues/119#issuecomment-4768185893". Furthermore, the Neuter Gender "kuṇḍagolam" is questionable in reference to the first definition "gruel". Please consider the following: "Treating "gruel" as a neuter substantive is morphologically valid for a foodstuff. However, comparative lexicography reveals inconsistency across standard reference works. Monier-Williams, for instance, records this exact meaning ("sour rice gruel") under the masculine nominal stem kuṇḍagolaḥ. Other traditional lexicons like the Śabdasāgara also align with the masculine for this specific meaning. LB •  Verdict: While not strictly a grammatical "error" by Apte, defining it exclusively as neuter requires a cautionary note, as the tradition widely attests to the masculine usage for the "gruel" definition."

Thus, a kāmin is fundamentally "one who is characterized by desire or deep affection." LB Contextual Meaning: When used specifically as a noun to describe a husband, it denotes a man who is excessively fond of, doting upon, or entirely captivated by his wife. LB 3. English Lexical Analysis ("Uxorious" vs. "Luxurious") LB Uxorious: This English adjective strictly means "having or showing an excessive or submissive fondness for one's wife." This is an exact, structurally precise translation of kāmin in this specific marital context. LB Luxurious: In standard English, this means characterized by opulence, wealth, or extreme comfort. While "luxurious" possessed an archaic, 17th-century meaning of "lecherous" or "lustful" (derived from Latin luxuria), it still lacked the specific conjugal focus required here. Furthermore, Apte already listed "lustful" as definition 1, making "luxurious" entirely redundant if taken in its archaic sense.
