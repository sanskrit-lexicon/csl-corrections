comment = Typo – Please remove the "comma". Here is the reason: "Removing the comma tightens the definition and actually makes it a more accurate reflection of the Sanskrit compound's structure. Here is the linguistic breakdown of why "a dark watery cloud" is superior: LB 1. English Grammar: Coordinate vs. Cumulative Adjectives LB With the comma (Coordinate): "A dark, watery cloud" treats "dark" and "watery" as coordinate adjectives. This implies they modify "cloud" equally and independently (i.e., "a cloud that is dark and watery"). LB Without the comma (Cumulative): "A dark watery cloud" treats them as cumulative adjectives. In this structure, "watery cloud" acts as a single, unified noun phrase, and "dark" modifies that entire phrase. LB 2. Alignment with Sanskrit Morphology LB The word kālābhra is a karmadhāraya compound consisting of kāla (dark/black) + abhra (cloud). LB If we look at the traditional etymology (such as in Yāska's Nirukta), abhra is derived as a water-bearing entity (apām bharaṇa). While it translates simply as "cloud," the inherent implication is a rain-cloud. LB If Vaidya's intention by writing "watery cloud" was to capture the specific nature of abhra, then "watery cloud" is the substantive base, and kāla is the adjective modifying it. LB Conclusion LB By dropping the comma, you perfectly mirror the karmadhāraya relationship in English: kāla (dark) directly modifies the base concept of abhra (watery cloud). It reads less like a loose list of attributes and more like a precise, unified compound. LB For your dictionary integration, "a dark watery cloud" (or simply "a dark rain-cloud") is the stronger, more structurally accurate choice."

According to standard grammar (Pāṇini 2.4.26: paraval liṅgaṃ dvandvatatpuruṣayoḥ), a Tatpuruṣa compound must retain the gender of its final member. Since vāhana (vehicle/instrument of carrying) is strictly neuter, the grammatically sound compound must be kumāravāhanam. LB The Bahuvrīhi Route (Absurd Meaning): If one were to strictly justify Apte’s masculine ending grammatically, it would have to be read as a Bahuvrīhi compound (which takes its gender from an external referent). LB How the Error Occurred LB Apte’s editors likely assigned the masculine ending simply because the implied substantive—the peacock (mayūraḥ or śikhī)—is a masculine noun. They casually conflated the semantic referent with the strict structural morphology of the compound itself. LB By simply swapping the masculine visarga (ḥ) for the neuter (m), you instantly resolve the morphological contradiction.
